# Hyperthyroidism and Kidney Disease in Cats — two diseases that hide each other

> Generated 2026-07-20 · PubMed search + verbatim verification · **Not clinical guidance.** Treatment decisions, dosing and monitoring belong to the attending veterinarian.
> **Why this file exists**: these are the two commonest chronic diseases of older cats, they occur together, and each one *conceals* the other. An endocrine specialist watching thyroid hormone and a nephrologist watching creatinine can both be reading their own numbers correctly while the cat's actual situation sits between them. That is the gap this project was built for — see `evidence-to-practice-gap.md`.

---

## 1. The mechanism: why the kidney disease is invisible until it is treated

Hyperthyroidism **"can complicate (mask) the diagnosis of chronic kidney disease (CKD) because it increases glomerular filtration rate and decreases body muscle mass, both of which can lower serum creatinine concentrations"** (Peterson 2018, PMID 29377360).

Both effects push creatinine down, and creatinine is the number most often used to rule kidney disease in or out. **So a hyperthyroid cat can have entirely normal kidney values and still have CKD underneath.** Treating the thyroid removes the mask.

**How often**: of 262 hyperthyroid cats, **none were azotaemic before treatment**, but **42 (16%) became azotaemic when rechecked 4–8 months (median 6) after radioiodine** (Peterson 2018).

> **⚠️ This is the single most useful thing on this page for an owner facing a treatment decision.** Roughly one in six cats will look like they have "developed kidney disease because of the treatment". In most cases the treatment did not cause it — it stopped hiding it. That distinction changes how the news lands, and it is precisely the kind of thing that gets lost when the thyroid result and the kidney result are read by different people on different days.

---

## 2. Nobody can reliably predict which cats

**"Currently, there is no clinical test that can reliably predict which hyperthyroid cats have concurrent azotemic CKD that will become apparent after treatment of the hyperthyroidism"** (Peterson 2018).

The best-studied candidate marker performs asymmetrically. As a test for masked CKD, serum SDMA showed **sensitivity 33.3% and specificity 97.7%** — the authors' own reading: **"The test has high diagnostic test specificity (few false-positive results) but relatively low sensitivity (fails to predict azotemia in most hyperthyroid cats)."**

> **How to use an asymmetric test, since this is where such numbers are routinely misread**: a *high* SDMA in an untreated hyperthyroid cat is meaningful — false positives are rare. A *normal* SDMA means very little, because the test misses two-thirds of the cats who will go on to become azotaemic. **A normal result is not reassurance.** Only 14 of the 42 cats who later became azotaemic had high pre-treatment SDMA.

---

## 3. The trap on the other side: overshooting the thyroid

Treatment aims to lower thyroid hormone. Pushed too far, it creates iatrogenic hypothyroidism — and that is not a benign landing place.

In 80 treated cats, azotaemia was significantly more common in the hypothyroid group (**16 of 28**) than the euthyroid group (**14 of 47**; P = .028). And the survival difference is stark: hypothyroid cats that became azotaemic had **median survival 456 days (range 231–1589)** versus **905 days (range 316–1869)** in those that stayed non-azotaemic (P = .018) (Williams 2010, PMID 20695989).

The authors' conclusion: **"Iatrogenic hypothyroidism appears to contribute to the development of azotemia after treatment of hyperthyroidism, and reduced survival time in azotemic cats."**

**A practical detail with a monitoring consequence**: **28 of 41 cats (68%)** whose total T4 fell below the reference range had raised TSH — so a low T4 alone does not establish hypothyroidism, and TSH is doing real work here. ⚠️ Which tests to run, and when, is the clinician's call; this is recorded so the question can be asked, not answered here.

---

## 4. Supplementation helps — but the window matters

In 117 radioiodine-treated cats, levothyroxine supplementation in hypothyroid **non-azotaemic** cats was associated with longer survival: **1037 days (300–2401) versus 768 days (34–1014)**, P = .027.

In hypothyroid cats that were **already azotaemic**, it was not: **771 days (718–1558) versus 152 days (82–1852)**, **P = .991** (Bree 2025, PMID 39831449).

> **∴ The benefit sits before azotaemia appears, not after.** Read together with §1 — where azotaemia surfaces at a median of six months post-treatment — this makes the post-treatment monitoring period the interval in which the decision can still be made. That is an integration point, not a new finding: §1 and §4 come from different papers, different years and different research groups, and neither states the combined implication.

⚠️ **The largest caveat in this file.** In that study, **"LT4 supplementation was offered to all hypothyroid cats with decision to treat based on owner preference."** Owners who chose supplementation may differ systematically from those who did not — in finances, in engagement, in how closely the cat was monitored. This is not a randomised comparison, and the survival difference may partly reflect *which owners said yes*. Treat it as supporting a hypothesis, not settling it.

**Baseline for context**, from the same study: euthyroid azotaemic cats **934 days (759–2035)** and non-supplemented hypothyroid cats **1232 days (238–2363)** both had shorter survival than euthyroid non-azotaemic cats **1616 days (663–3369)** (P = .003 and P = .002).

## 5. What this means for how the two diseases are followed

**This section is interpretation, not a finding.** It is the connection the source papers do not draw, offered so an owner can ask about it.

1. **Normal kidney values before treatment do not mean healthy kidneys.** §1 gives the mechanism; roughly one in six will show azotaemia afterwards.
2. **A normal SDMA is not reassurance**, though a high one is informative (§2).
3. **"Kidney disease appeared after treatment" is usually unmasking, not damage** — a distinction that matters for what is decided next.
4. **Overshooting into hypothyroidism carries a measurable cost** (§3), so the target is a range rather than "as low as possible".
5. **If hypothyroidism occurs, the interval before azotaemia is when supplementation has support** (§4) — and that interval is roughly the same six months in which azotaemia typically surfaces.

⚠️ Every one of these is a question to put to the attending veterinarian, who has the examination, the trend over time, and this cat's other conditions. **None of it is a protocol, and none of it should override a clinician's plan.**

---

## 6. Gaps worth closing next

- Whether earlier detection of masked CKD changes outcomes, rather than merely predicting them. No study here tests that.
- A randomised comparison of levothyroxine supplementation, which the owner-preference design in Bree 2025 cannot substitute for.
- Methimazole and dietary iodine restriction: this file covers radioiodine-treated populations almost exclusively, because that is what the retrieved literature studies. Whether the same unmasking dynamics and thresholds apply to medically managed cats is **not established here**.
- Cats with concurrent hypertension, which frequently accompanies both diseases and is absent from this file entirely.

# Feline Lymphoma Treatment — a currency check, and the 2025 options the textbooks predate

> Generated 2026-07-23 · PubMed search + verbatim verification · Not diagnostic, not a prescription
> **Purpose**: this file exists because a chemotherapy protocol circulating in an owner community was checked against the published feline evidence and found to be a **faithful digest of standard veterinary oncology** — the right drugs, the cat-correct details. That is the reassuring half. The other half is the one this project keeps re-learning: **a protocol that matches a textbook is proof of copying, not of currency.** A source's date is part of its evidence. This file records what a *forward* read of the literature adds that an older digest cannot contain.
> **What it deliberately does NOT do**: it does not restate the standard protocols or their doses — those, with their toxicity profiles, already live in [`feline-oncology-literature-survey.md`](feline-oncology-literature-survey.md) §3 and [`antineoplastic-drug-toxicity.md`](antineoplastic-drug-toxicity.md), verbatim-sourced. And it is **not a dosing sheet**: chemotherapy dosing, sequencing and monitoring belong to the attending oncologist. Read this as *what to know is current*, not *what to give*.

## 0. The currency verdict

### 0.1 The standard protocols still stand — that part of an old digest is safe

As of 2025 the core strategy is unchanged and independently re-confirmed: cell size is the first fork; **small-cell alimentary lymphoma is treated with oral chlorambucil + prednisolone** and does well, while **high-grade/large-cell disease is treated with a multi-agent COP/CHOP-class protocol** and does far less well. The most recent direct comparison (123 cats, three hospitals) still puts high-grade response at 66% and median survival around three months, with mitoxantrone-based CMOP equivalent to doxorubicin-based CHOP — the numbers and the sources are in [`feline-oncology-literature-survey.md`](feline-oncology-literature-survey.md) §3 (Lai 2025, PMID 40443182, already archived). **So a circulating digest built on chlorambucil + prednisolone for small-cell and CHOP/CMOP for large-cell is not out of date on its backbone.** The cat-specific safety facts that such digests most often get wrong — **prednisolone, not prednisone; doxorubicin's target organ in the cat is the kidney, not the heart; a CBC before each myelosuppressive dose** — are likewise already in [`antineoplastic-drug-toxicity.md`](antineoplastic-drug-toxicity.md).

> **⭐ Why this file still needs to exist.** Everything in §0.1 could be reconstructed from a 2015-era review. The two findings below could not — they are 2025, prospective or purpose-collected, and they change what an owner can be *offered*, not just what the prognosis is. Matching the old source would have hidden them. This is the "教科书可能老了" lesson as a concrete gap, not a slogan.

## 1. What a forward read adds (2025)

### 1.1 Small-cell / CIE-complex disease now has a non-drug option — low-dose abdominal radiation

For the indolent small-cell end — specifically the **lymphocytic lymphoma / chronic inflammatory enteropathy complex (FLL/CIE)** — a 2025 prospective pilot delivered **low-dose abdominal cavity radiation (8 Gy total, in four 2 Gy fractions), with no steroids or chemotherapy first** (Gieger 2025, PMID 40726309). Of 14 cats enrolled and 13 completed, *"Nine of the 12 remaining cats (75%… ) had a clinical benefit to treatment… that was sustained for > 340 days"*, and six were *"asymptomatic … or stable … at a median of 635 days after RT (range, 447-1014 days)."* Nearly all had a **transient GI flare in the first 1-3 weeks**.

> **Why this matters to an owner, and its limits.** The authors frame the niche precisely: RT *"could be considered for cats that cannot tolerate steroids and/or for owners that cannot pill cats routinely."* That is a real subset — the indolent form is otherwise a lifelong twice-daily pilling commitment. ⚠️ But this is a **14-cat single-arm pilot**, not a comparison against chlorambucil + prednisolone (which already delivers multi-year survival, §0.1). It expands the menu; it does not displace the standard. Radiation dosing and delivery are a radiation oncologist's, full stop.

### 1.2 CNS lymphoma — a form with almost no prior evidence — now has a described option

Feline **CNS lymphoma** has historically had little to guide treatment. A 2025 retrospective describes **lomustine + corticosteroids** in 10 cats: *"Seven cats (70%) were still alive at the 3-month follow-up, with 3 (30%) maintaining improvement at 6 and 12 months,"* and *"The treatment did not result in any significant adverse effect"* (Pauciulo 2025, PMID 41072475). The authors' own conclusion is deliberately modest: it is *"a valid chemotherapeutic option,"* with *"Larger prospective studies … warranted."*

> **How to hold this.** For a diagnosis that previously left owners with almost nothing published, "a valid option in 10 cats, 70% alive at 3 months" is genuinely new information — and equally, it is 10 cats, retrospective, no comparator. ⚠️ **Lomustine is the most myelosuppressive of the oral agents in this knowledge base** (dose-limiting; delayed 7-28 day nadir — see [`antineoplastic-drug-toxicity.md`](antineoplastic-drug-toxicity.md)), so it is emphatically a clinician-run drug with CBC monitoring. This file records that the option now exists and is sourced; it does not put a dose to it, because the abstract does not, and this project does not supply doses from recall.

## 2. At the decision moment

- **A shared protocol matching the textbook backbone is reassuring, not sufficient.** Check it on three cat-specific axes it commonly fails (prednisolone vs prednisone; doxorubicin-as-nephrotoxin; pre-dose CBC) — all in [`antineoplastic-drug-toxicity.md`](antineoplastic-drug-toxicity.md) — and then ask the oncologist whether anything **newer** applies to *this* cat's form. As of 2025 that "newer" list includes abdominal RT for the small-cell/CIE end (§1.1) and lomustine + corticosteroids for the CNS form (§1.2).
- **Neither 2025 option is a reason to change a working plan.** Both are small, single-arm/retrospective, and sit alongside — not above — the established protocols in §0.1. Their value is for the cats the standard path does not fit: can't tolerate steroids, can't be pilled, or a form with no prior evidence at all.
- **This file supplies no doses and no sequencing.** That is the attending oncologist's, and the emergency screen still runs first regardless of any of the above.

## 原文摘录（source excerpts）

> The sentences below are **verbatim excerpts from the source literature**, untranslated.
> The prose in the body above is my interpretation; **if you need to cite, cite the original sentences here**, and go back to the source to check the context.
> Only the two 2025 papers unique to this file are excerpted here; the core-protocol sources are excerpted in the two files cross-referenced above, not duplicated.

**PMID 40726309** · Gieger TL 2025
> The goal of this prospective, single-arm pilot study was to assess tolerability and clinical benefit for cats with histologically confirmed lymphocytic lymphoma/chronic inflammatory enteropathy complex (FLL/CIE) treated with low-dose abdominal cavity radiation therapy (RT; 8 Gy total dose administered in four 2 Gy fractions).
> No cats received steroids or chemotherapy prior to RT.
> Fourteen cats were enrolled and 13 completed the study.
> Nine of the 12 remaining cats (75%; N = 6 with FLL and N = 3 with CIE) had a clinical benefit to treatment (resolution or improvement of GI signs as defined by owner surveys and body weight) that was sustained for > 340 days.
> Six cats remained asymptomatic (N = 5) or stable (N = 1) at a median of 635 days after RT (range, 447-1014 days).
> Low-dose abdominal cavity RT could be considered for cats that cannot tolerate steroids and/or for owners that cannot pill cats routinely.

**PMID 41072475** · Pauciulo C 2025
> To describe the clinical outcomes of 10 cats diagnosed with CNS lymphoma treated with lomustine and corticosteroids.
> Seven cats (70%) were still alive at the 3-month follow-up, with 3 (30%) maintaining improvement at 6 and 12 months.
> The treatment did not result in any significant adverse effect.
> The combination of lomustine and corticosteroids is a valid chemotherapeutic option in cats with CNS lymphoma. Larger prospective studies are warranted to compare efficacy of this treatment with that of other chemotherapeutic protocols.

# Feline Chronic Kidney Disease: What Is Established, What Is Only Predicted, and What an Owner Decides

> **Generated**: 2026-07-20 **Method**: PubMed sweep across four angles (diet/therapy trials, prognostic factors, SDMA and early detection, owner burden and quality of life), screened locally, eleven papers archived and quoted verbatim, joined to the population data in `feline-disease-frequency.md`.
> **Why this file exists**: CKD is 25× more common than the lymphoma this repository was built around, is roughly a tenth of all feline deaths, and its evidence is split across nephrology, nutrition, endocrinology and hypertension literatures — an integration gap, not a data gap (`docs/LITERATURE-PIPELINE-SOP.md` §7a).
> ⚠️ **Nothing here is a treatment plan.** Staging, drug choice, diet transition and fluid decisions belong to the attending veterinarian, who has examined the cat. This file exists so an owner can follow that conversation and ask about the things the population data say usually go unasked.

---

## 0. The whole thing in one sentence

**Diet is the one intervention with strong survival evidence, and it is strong only in cats that are already azotaemic; everything upstream of that — earlier detection, earlier intervention — is at present a prediction that has not been shown to pay off.**

---

## 1. The starting position, from the population

From `feline-disease-frequency.md` (PMID 31023949, 353,448 cats in UK primary care):

- Prevalence **1.2%**; **66.6% already had clinical signs at diagnosis**
- Median survival after diagnosis **388 days** (IQR 88-1042) — note the interquartile range spans **three months to nearly three years**
- Monitored: creatinine **32.6%**, UPC **14.9%**, blood pressure **25.6%**
- A renal diet was the most frequently prescribed management (**63.8%**)

⚠️ **That IQR is the most useful number in this file for an owner.** "About a year" is the median and describes almost nobody. CKD at diagnosis is not one prognosis; §2 is about what separates the ends of that range.

---

## 2. What actually predicts survival

In 95 placebo-group cats followed up to 1,097 days within a benazepril trial (PMID 17939542):

**Independent risk factors** — each carried information the others did not:
- increased plasma **creatinine**
- increased **urine protein-to-creatinine ratio (UPC)**
- increased blood **leukocyte count**

**Dependent risk factors** — significant, but correlated with baseline creatinine, so not adding independent information: plasma phosphate, urea, and lower haemoglobin or haematocrit.

⚠️ **The independent/dependent distinction is the whole point of that paper and is routinely lost when it is cited.** Phosphate and anaemia matter clinically; what this analysis says is that they did not predict survival *beyond* what creatinine already told you. That is a statement about prediction, not about whether treating them helps.

⚠️ **Read the endpoint before reading the survival.** "Renal survival time" here ends at *the need for parenteral fluid therapy, euthanasia, or death* — and of 37 cats reaching it, **0 died**; 17 were euthanised, 12 started parenteral fluids, 8 both. **This is a composite endpoint dominated by human decisions**, so it measures the course of the illness and the owner's and clinician's thresholds together. That is not a flaw — it is what "survival" means in a disease where the ending is nearly always chosen — but it must not be read as biological survival.

**⭐ UPC is an independent predictor and is measured in 14.9% of cats.** Of the three independent factors, one is on every biochemistry panel, one is on every haematology panel, and the third requires a urine sample nobody collected in six cases out of seven.

## 3. Diet: the one intervention with robust evidence

A 2009 systematic review appraised the whole therapeutic repertoire — "fluid therapy, calcitrioi therapy, antihypertensive therapy, ACE inhibitor therapy, erythropoietic hormone replacement therapy, potassium supplementation, antioxidant supplementation, alkalinization therapy, dietary phosphorus restriction and intestinal phosphate binders, therapeutic renal foods, assisted feeding, dialysis and renal transplantation" — and found that "evidence of efficacy or effectiveness is often lacking or highly variable" (PMID 19237135).

Sixteen years later the position is the same and is stated plainly: **"dietary modification (particularly protein- and phosphorus-restriction) in cats with azotaemic CKD is the only intervention demonstrating robust evidence for improving survival times and slowing disease progression"** (PMID 40763836, 2025).

Renal diets in IRIS stages 2-4 improve survival, reduce uraemic crises, and maintain body weight and body condition for up to 2 years, and the argument for higher-protein diets has "no convincing evidence in support" (PMID 27485277).

⚠️ **"The only intervention with robust evidence" is not "the only intervention that works."** It is a statement about the evidence base, not about the biology. Antihypertensives, phosphate binders and potassium supplementation are standard care for good reasons; what the reviews record is that the trials establishing survival benefit have mostly not been done.

## 4. ⭐ The early-detection chain, and the two links that do not hold

The argument for finding CKD sooner has three links: **detect earlier → intervene earlier → live longer**. Each link now has evidence, and they do not currently connect.

### 4.1 Link one — detection. SDMA is not established

A systematic review of SDMA's diagnostic accuracy found **eleven studies, five in cats (481 animals)**, with "marked heterogeneity in the design, population and tests used". **Nine of eleven were at high risk of bias, and nine had high applicability concerns.** Heterogeneity precluded meta-analysis. Its conclusion is blunt: **"The use of SDMA may increase the risk of misdiagnosis and overdiagnosis of CKD"** (PMID 41527203).

⚠️ **Overdiagnosis is a specific harm, not a synonym for a wrong result.** It means correctly identifying something that was never going to hurt the cat — and then acting on it: a diet change, repeat visits, an owner who now has a sick cat where before they had a cat. In a disease whose only proven intervention is a food the cat may refuse, that cost is not hypothetical.

### 4.2 Link two — prediction. This one holds

In 201 healthy cats aged ≥7, screened every 6 months for 2 years (PMID 39696777), cats with borderline proteinuria (UPC 0.2-0.4) at inclusion were significantly more likely to develop renal disease (log-rank *P* = .004) or die (*P* = .02) than non-proteinuric cats. In multivariable analysis, IRIS stage 1 CKD (HR 4.2, 95% CI 2.0-8.8), creatinine ≥1.6 mg/dL (HR 2.6, 95% CI 1.1-6.4), borderline proteinuria (HR 2.5, 95% CI 1.2-5.2) and age (HR 1.3, 95% CI 1.2-1.5) were each associated with a diagnosis of renal disease six months later.

**So non-azotaemic cats can be risk-stratified prospectively.** That is a real, prospectively-collected result, and it is the strongest link in the chain.

### 4.3 Link three — benefit. This is where it breaks

The one study addressing it directly: 1,430 cats with early CKD, 839 on a therapeutic renal diet and 591 not, reporting **45% lower hazard of progression in stage 1** (HR 0.55, 95% CI 0.52-0.58), similar reductions in stage 2, and **restricted mean survival of 31.0 versus 26.0 months** over 3 years (PMID 41534199, 2026).

⚠️ **This is retrospective database work, and confounding by indication here is severe rather than incidental.** Cats were not randomised. A cat "continuously treated with a veterinary therapeutic renal diet" is a cat that (a) has an owner who bought it, kept buying it, and returned to the practice, and (b) **would eat it.** Appetite is not a nuisance variable in CKD — it is one of the disease's central features, it tracks with quality of life (§6), and inappetence is a hallmark of progression. **The exposure is partly a measurement of the outcome.**

⚠️ **The confidence intervals invite a second look.** HR 0.55 with a 95% CI of 0.52-0.58, from 1,430 cats, is an interval far tighter than that sample would ordinarily produce for a survival hazard. It is a consequence of the modelling approach the authors used (longitudinal targeted maximum likelihood estimation), **and precision of that kind narrows the random error, not the confounding.** Do not read the tight interval as strength of evidence.

**Where that leaves it.** The 2026 study is newer than the 2025 review that says robust evidence exists only for azotaemic cats — but newer is not stronger, and a retrospective cohort does not overturn the standard the review was applying. **The honest summary: prediction in non-azotaemic cats is established; benefit from acting on that prediction is suggested by one confounded study and is not established.**

⚠️ **This is the same question `feline-hypertension.md`, `hyperthyroidism-and-kidney-disease.md` and `emergency-triage-red-flags.md` each failed to answer, appearing for the fourth time.** Recording that it is unanswered *again*, with the specific reason it is unanswered here, is the finding.

## 5. The hypertension join

CKD and hypertension have, in the reviewers' phrase, "an intermingled cause-and-effect relationship"; hypertension "also drives proteinuria, which is an independent risk factor for progression and mortality in cats with CKD"; and current guidelines target systolic pressure **<160 mmHg** (PMID 34428941).

The review states the practice gap directly: **"blood pressure monitoring in cats with CKD is still infrequently performed, and hypertension likely remains underdiagnosed in this population."**

**⭐ Three files now converge on one number.**

| Source | What it records |
|---|---|
| `feline-hypertension.md` | 96.5% of clinics own a direct ophthalmoscope; **73.1% of practitioners struggle to interpret ocular findings** |
| PMID 34428941 (this file) | BP monitoring in CKD "still infrequently performed"; hypertension "likely remains underdiagnosed" |
| PMID 31023949 (`feline-disease-frequency.md`) | **25.6%** of cats diagnosed with CKD had blood pressure measured |

The review asserts underdiagnosis; the primary-care data quantify it; the survey supplies a mechanism. **None of the three papers contains the other two's half.**

⚠️ **And the loop does not close.** The same review states that the "effect of hypertension and antihypertensive treatment on mortality and progression of CKD" remains unclear. So: the association is well evidenced, the measurement usually does not happen, and **whether treating it changes the outcome is not established.** All three must be said together — the first alone overstates, the third alone would wrongly imply measurement does not matter.

## 6. Quality of life, and what is modifiable

In 68 cats with CKD versus 24 healthy cats, using a validated instrument (PMID 40937630):

- CKD cats scored lower on **vitality, comfort and emotional wellbeing** (all *P* <0.0001)
- IRIS stage 3-4 scored lower than stage 1-2 on emotional wellbeing (*P* = 0.04) and vitality (*P* = 0.04)
- **Anaemia**: lower emotional wellbeing (25.9 vs 46.6, *P* = 0.005), with haematocrit positively correlated (*r* = 0.33)
- **Abnormal appetite**: lower emotional wellbeing (29.1 vs 48.1, *P* = 0.001)
- **Constipation**: lower comfort (*P* = 0.0003) and emotional wellbeing (*P* = 0.008)

**⭐ Constipation is the one worth pulling out.** It is unglamorous, it is treatable, and it carried the largest comfort difference in the study. An owner watching for "kidney symptoms" is watching for drinking and weight; **the litter tray is where a modifiable source of discomfort shows up first.**

⚠️ **Cross-sectional and correlational.** Nothing here shows that treating anaemia, appetite or constipation raises quality of life — only that cats with those problems scored lower. The authors' own phrasing is "associated with", and the sample is 68 cats. Treat this as a list of things worth asking about, not as a demonstrated intervention.

## 7. What the owner is actually doing: subcutaneous fluids

A survey of 468 CKD cat owners, 399 of whom gave subcutaneous fluids at home (PMID 28948902):

- **85%** found it easy, somewhat easy or okay for themselves; **89%** reported the same for their cat
- **95%** had discussed it with their veterinarian, but **only 42% of those discussions involved additional educational resources**
- Tolerance strategies owners reported: a treat afterwards (57%), warming the fluids (60%), needle gauge (61% used 20 G or larger; 49% said gauge affected tolerance), and duration (74%)
- **40%** monitored hydration, and of those, **40% skipped or added fluids** based on their assessment

⚠️ **A self-selected online survey of owners who were already doing it.** Owners who tried and stopped, or never started, are largely absent — so the 85%/89% figures describe the people who persisted, not the average owner's experience, and cannot be quoted as "most owners find this easy". The direction is still worth having: **it is a learnable skill and the people doing it mostly cope.**

⚠️ **The 40%-of-40% figure is the one to be careful about.** Roughly one owner in six was adjusting fluid volumes on their own hydration assessment. The survey reports this; it does not evaluate whether those adjustments were correct, and **this file is not a licence to do it.** Volume and frequency are the veterinarian's prescription. The useful reading is the gap it exposes: **more than half of owners got no educational material beyond a conversation**, which is a fixable thing to ask for.

## 9. Genuine gaps

- **Does earlier detection improve outcome?** Unanswered, for the fourth time in this repository. §4 gives the specific reason it is unanswered for CKD: the prediction is sound and the intervention evidence at that stage is confounded.
- **Does antihypertensive treatment change CKD progression or mortality?** Stated as unclear by PMID 34428941. Not searched exhaustively here.
- **Is there any feline evidence on when to stop?** Not searched. The composite endpoint in §2 shows euthanasia timing is doing much of the work in every survival figure in this file, and this repository has nothing on how that decision is made in CKD specifically.
- **Renal diet acceptance.** Every diet result assumes the cat eats the food. No study found here measures what fraction refuse it, which is the first question an owner actually faces.
- **Non-UK, non-North American data.** As with `feline-disease-frequency.md`, essentially everything above is UK or North American.

## 原文摘录（source excerpts）

**PMID 17939542** · King JN 2007
> Increased plasma creatinine concentration, increased urine protein-to-creatinine ratio (UPC), and increased blood leukocyte count were significantly (P < .01) associated with a shorter renal survival time and were independent risk factors.
> Increased concentrations of plasma phosphate or urea, and lower blood hemoglobin concentration or hematocrit were significantly (P < .01) associated with a shorter renal survival time and were dependent risk factors, because they also were significantly (P < .01) correlated with plasma creatinine concentration at baseline.
> Renal survival time was defined as the time from initiation of therapy to the need for parenteral fluid therapy, euthanasia, or death related to renal failure.
> Of the 95 cats treated with a placebo, 58 were censored and 37 reached the renal survival end point (died, n = 0; euthanized, n = 17; parenteral fluids, n = 12; parenteral fluids followed by euthanasia, n = 8).
> ⚠️ The placebo arm of a benazepril trial, not a general CKD cohort: entry required creatinine ≥2.0 mg/dL and urine specific gravity ≤1.025. The endpoint is composite and decision-dominated — zero cats died of the disease.

**PMID 19237135** · Roudebush P 2009
> Over the years, many therapeutic and preventive interventions have been developed or advocated for chronic kidney disease (CKD), but evidence of efficacy or effectiveness is often lacking or highly variable.
> Accordingly, the main objective of this systematic review was to identify and critically appraise the evidence supporting various approaches to managing feline CKD; namely, fluid therapy, calcitrioi therapy, antihypertensive therapy, ACE inhibitor therapy, erythropoietic hormone replacement therapy, potassium supplementation, antioxidant supplementation, alkalinization therapy, dietary phosphorus restriction and intestinal phosphate binders, therapeutic renal foods, assisted feeding, dialysis and renal transplantation.
> ⚠️ "calcitrioi" is a typo in the published abstract for calcitriol, quoted here as printed rather than silently corrected.
> ⚠️ 2009. Cited for the shape of the evidence base, which PMID 40763836 confirms is largely unchanged in 2025 — not for any individual therapy's current status.

**PMID 26936494** · Sparkes AH 2016
> Chronic kidney disease (CKD) is one of the most commonly diagnosed diseases in older cats.
> In addition to addressing problems arising from CKD and improving quality of life (QoL) for the patient, therapy may also target slowing the underlying progression of disease and hence prolonging life.
> While maintaining QoL is of paramount importance in our patients, this can be challenging when multiple therapies are indicated.
> In some cases it is necessary to prioritise therapy, given an understanding of what is likely to most benefit the individual patient.
> ⚠️ Consensus guidelines, not a study — the same document class as `feline-hypertension.md`'s ISFM source, and read the same way: the field's best current position, expert judgement included. The Panel graded evidence quality per intervention; those gradings are in the full text and are **not** retrieved here.

**PMID 27485277** · Polzin DJ 2016
> Clinical trials in cats with CKD have shown them to be effective in improving survival, reducing uremic crises, and improving serum urea nitrogen and phosphorous concentrations.
> It has shown that, when food intake is adequate, renal diets can maintain body weight and body condition scores for up to 2 years.
> Although some have questioned whether renal diets provide adequate protein and have advocated feeding higher-protein diets to cats with CKD, there is currently no convincing evidence in support of this proposal.
> ⚠️ **Explicitly one side of a designated controversy** — the title says "The Pro View", and a Con View was published alongside it and is **not** retrieved here. Cited as the affirmative case, not as a neutral review. Note the conditional the author states himself: "when food intake is adequate".

**PMID 28948902** · Cooley CM 2018
> Owners of 468 cats with CKD participated, 399 of whom administered SC fluids.
> Ninety-five percent of owners said they discussed giving fluids with their veterinarian, with only 42% of those discussions involving additional educational resources.
> A large majority of owners (85%) said it was either an easy, somewhat easy/no stress or okay experience for them, and a large majority (89%) reported that the experience was easy/no stress, somewhat easy or okay experience for their cats.
> Hydration status was monitored by 40% of owners by various methods, with 40% of those saying they skipped or added fluids based on hydration assessment.
> Overall, the protocol should be tailored to the preference of the cat for best possible long-term success.
> ⚠️ Self-selected web survey advertised online, restricted to owners already giving fluids. Those who tried and stopped are structurally absent, so the tolerance figures describe persisters.

**PMID 34428941** · Lawson JS 2021
> Hypertension also drives proteinuria, which is an independent risk factor for progression and mortality in cats with CKD.
> Current guidelines recommend a target systolic blood pressure of <160 mmHg to minimise risk of TOD.
> Despite this, blood pressure monitoring in cats with CKD is still infrequently performed, and hypertension likely remains underdiagnosed in this population.
> Clinical signs of hypertension may not be apparent to owners of affected cats until severe hypertensive TOD is present.
> However, significant aspects, such as the mechanisms behind this association, and effect of hypertension and antihypertensive treatment on mortality and progression of CKD, remain unclear.
> ⚠️ Narrative review of published literature, not primary data. "Infrequently performed" is its assertion; the 25.6% figure quantifying it comes from PMID 31023949 in a different file.

**PMID 39696777** · Mortier F 2025
> A total of 201 healthy client-owned cats ≥7 years of age; 150 nonproteinuric (urinary protein : creatinine ratio [UPC] <0.2) and 51 borderline proteinuric (UPC 0.2-0.4).
> Significantly more cats with borderline proteinuria at inclusion developed renal disease (International Renal Interest Society [IRIS] ≥ stage 2 CKD or renal proteinuria; log-rank P = .004) or died (log-rank P = .02) within 2 years, compared with nonproteinuric cats.
> In the multivariate analysis, IRIS stage 1 CKD (persistent USG <1.035 or sSDMA >14 μg/dL; hazard ratio [HR], 4.2; 95% confidence interval [CI], 2.0-8.8; P < .001), sCrea ≥1.6 mg/dL (≥140 μmol/L; HR, 2.6; 95% CI, 1.1-6.4; P = .04), borderline proteinuria (HR, 2.5; 95% CI, 1.2-5.2; P = .01), and age at inclusion (HR, 1.3; 95% CI, 1.2-1.5; P < .001) were significantly associated with diagnosis of renal disease 6 months later.
> Borderline proteinuria should receive more attention in healthy mature adult and senior cats because it is associated with renal disease and death.
> ⚠️ Prospective, 6-monthly screening, 2 years — the strongest design in this file. It establishes **prediction only**; no intervention was assigned or tested.
> ⚠️ Same first author as the 259-cat senior screening cohort cited in `feline-oncology-literature-survey.md` (PMID 38967102). Overlapping research programme; the cohorts should not be treated as independent replications of each other.

**PMID 40763836** · Bestwick JP 2025
> To date, however, dietary modification (particularly protein- and phosphorus-restriction) in cats with azotaemic CKD is the only intervention demonstrating robust evidence for improving survival times and slowing disease progression.
> As weight loss and phosphorus dyshomeostasis are present in non-azotaemic cats, and progression to azotaemic CKD may occur over time, earlier diagnosis and management represent a compelling opportunity for optimising management of this disease.
> Increased awareness and availability of a novel biomarker for detecting glomerular filtration rate reduction (symmetric dimethylarginine), alongside efforts by the International Renal Interest Society to standardise diagnosis and staging, have led to earlier detection of CKD and an ability to study and trial interventions in sub-populations based on disease severity.
> ⚠️ Narrative review, and the authors are explicit that pre-azotaemic intervention is "a compelling opportunity" — i.e. a hypothesis. Do not cite the second sentence as evidence that early intervention works.

**PMID 40937630** · Lorbach SK 2025
> Data from 24 healthy cats and 68 cats with CKD were collected.
> Cats with anaemia had lower median EWB scores (25.9, range 10.4-54.4) than those without (46.6, range 1.6-58.8) (P = 0.005), and haematocrit was positively correlated with EWB (P = 0.005, r = 0.33).
> Cats with abnormal appetite had lower median EWB scores (29.1, range 1.6-56.7) than cats with normal appetite (48.1, range 11.2-58.8) (P = 0.001).
> Cats with constipation had lower median comfort scores 29.5 (range 21.2-59.5) (P = 0.0003) and lower median EWB scores (22, range 1.6-58.8) (P = 0.008) than those without (comfort: 37.1, range 25.3-59.6; EWB: 47.9, range 11.2-58.8).
> Cats with CKD have decreased HRQoL. Several modifiable clinical factors are associated with this decreased HRQoL.
> ⚠️ Caregiver-completed instrument, cross-sectional, 68 CKD cats. "Modifiable" is the authors' word for the factors; **no modification was performed or tested.**

**PMID 41527203** · Scobie C
> Eleven studies were included: five in cats (481 animals) and six in dogs (460 animals).
> Nine studies were at high risk of bias, and nine had high applicability concerns.
> There was marked variation in sensitivity and specificity.
> Heterogeneity and risk of bias present precluded meta-analysis.
> The use of SDMA may increase the risk of misdiagnosis and overdiagnosis of CKD.
> Significant uncertainty remains regarding the diagnostic accuracy of SDMA in CKD in cats and dogs.
> ⚠️ The authors record their own limitations: "Grey literature searching, data extraction and risk of bias assessment lacked independent duplication." Quoted because a review's own methodological caveat is evidence about the review.
> ⚠️ Cats and dogs pooled in the review; the feline evidence is five studies and 481 animals.

**PMID 41534199** · Coyne M 2026
> Of 1,430 cats with early CKD, 839 received a veterinary therapeutic renal diet and 591 did not.
> Treated CKD Stage 1 cats had a 45% lower hazard of progression (hazard ratio [HR], 0.55; 95% CI, 0.52 to 0.58).
> Cats treated with a therapeutic renal diet had a longer survival over 3 years: restricted mean survival time was 31.0 versus 26.0 months in untreated cats.
> This retrospective study utilized a commercial database of medical records from veterinary practices located in Canada and the US.
> Progression of CKD and survival duration were evaluated with longitudinal targeted maximum likelihood estimation modeling.
> ⚠️ **Retrospective; "randomly selected" refers to record selection for review, not to treatment allocation.** Confounding by indication is structural here — being continuously fed a therapeutic diet requires an owner who keeps buying it and a cat that will eat it, and appetite is itself a marker of CKD progression.
> ⚠️ The 95% CI of 0.52-0.58 around HR 0.55 is far tighter than 1,430 animals would ordinarily give. It reflects the estimator, and **narrow random error does not address confounding.**
